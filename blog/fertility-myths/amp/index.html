Debunking Fertility Myths

by Dr. Nataki Douglas

2018-11-16

Let's be real - misinformation about fertility is everywhere. Even if you think you're at the top of your reproductive health game, there are still probably one or two "facts" in your arsenal that, it turns out, aren't exactly correct. To help you get the right information, we asked Dr. Nataki Douglas, Head of the Modern Fertility Medical Advisory Board, to help us bust some stubborn myths around fertility.

  1. Getting your period every month means good fertility

ND: Unfortunately, this is not true. Getting your period at the same time every month, give or take 1-2 days, is a great indication that you are ovulating each month.

Ovulation is required for fertility, but fertility is more than just ovulation.

  1. Birth control harms your ability to conceive later

ND: Birth control has not been proven to have any harmful long-term effects on a woman’s ability to conceive. However, once you stop your birth control method and are ready to get pregnant, you want to pay close attention to the regularity of your menstrual cycles. Being on birth control can mask signs of a common reproductive disorder, called polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS). If your cycles do not become regular in 3-4 months after stopping your birth control, you may want to check-in with your doctor. If you are over 35 years old, it is not a bad idea to discuss your plans to stop your birth control with your Gyn at your next annual visit.

  1. If you have one successful pregnancy, you should have no problem getting pregnant again.

ND: Having a successful pregnancy does not guarantee another successful pregnancy. Many women/couples have what is called secondary infertility (The Modern Fertility Timeline Tool is a great way to get a baseline understanding of how fertility evolves with age.)

  1. Using Plan B makes you infertile

    ND: Using Plan B does not affect your fertility. Plan B is a huge dose of hormones used in many birth control pills that prevents fertilization of an egg or prevents the fertilized egg from attaching to the uterus. Once you get your period after taking Plan B, your cycle has restarted and the Plan B hormones are out of your system.

  2. If your mom had an easy time getting pregnant, you will too

    ND: Not a guarantee… The ability to get pregnant and/or how easy it will be to get pregnant is not completely inherited. Every woman is different. However, research has shown that some conditions that may make it more difficult to get pregnant, like PCOS and POI (premature ovarian insufficiency), have heritable genetic components.

  3. It's common to be able to have to kids at age 45 like "XYZ"celeb

    ND: Having kids at age 45 is possible, but challenging. The main challenge is that at age 45, a woman’s ovaries do not have many healthy eggs left. We are lucky in that a woman’s womb/uterus does not age the way the eggs do. So, even if you can’t get pregnant with your “45 year-old eggs”, you can successfully carry a pregnancy and have kids.
